My practice as a media artist, media designer and lecturer investigates how technologies function — and how, in functioning, they produce realities. Until now I have worked mostly one layer above_the silicon: with the systems, simulations and interfaces we encounter as "the digital," and with the conditions of perception, labour and control they shape. I work across media, but most often with 3D simulation, virtual and augmented reality, and creative coding.

Three recent works mark the territory. The One Best Way (2025) is a four-chapter mixed-media installation that traces the logic of labour optimisation from early-twentieth-century motion studies to contemporary Digital Twin factories — virtual replicas of industrial environments in which work is endlessly simulated, monitored and refined through machine learning. The work asks how the simulation itself becomes a new normative model, no longer derived from observing workers but prescribing how they should act. Blissful_(2023) is a machine-learning installation in which a computer reads CGI clouds drifting across a Windows XP horizon and tells stories about the figures it "recognises"; it stages the moments in which human and machine pattern recognition both fail, and in which we accept the machine's misreadings because they sound convincingly human. Illusion of the digital (2017, collaboration with Piet Schmidt), made earlier in dialogue with Claus Pias's writing on analog, digital and the cybernetic illusion, sets a phosphorescent rotating disk against a UV switch and a photosensor: the supposed cleanness of a binary signal is shown to be, materially, a continuous, decaying, analog event. Digitality, the work suggests, is a mental model that ignores the intermediate states in which it actually takes place.

It is precisely this last thread that the PDI residency would let me pursue at a depth my practice has not yet reached. So far I have approached technology from above the semiconductor layer; but i would like to dive deeper in what makes that layer possible. I would like to engage  with the processes of semiconductor fabrication and characterisation — molecular beam epitaxy, the clean room, spectroscopic measurement — to understand what infrastructures of materials, environments and instruments are required for what we casually call "artificial intelligence" or "the cloud" to exist at all.

What draws me most is a tension I keep circling and have no satisfying form for yet: the apparent immateriality of contemporary computation set against the immense materiality of its substrate — fabs, AI data centres, atomic-scale layers of crystal — which has itself become so vast and so complex that it begins, paradoxically, to slip back out of perceptual reach into its own kind of non-materiality. PDI would be a   place where one can think about that paradox from inside the apparatus that produces it, in conversation with the people who do.

I would arrive with that question, my tools (3D simulation, VR/AR, video, installation), and a willingness to let what I encounter complicate what I already think.
